## Abstract

This paper studies whether new urban transport infrastructure reshapes realised access to education. I examine the opening of the Elizabeth Line in London that substantially improved local transport accessibility from 2022. Using official school level data for public secondary schools in London from 2014 to 2025, I estimate a difference-in-differences model that compares changes in Key Stage 4 outcomes across schools with different proximity to the new line. I find that schools closer to Elizabeth Line stations experienced a significant increase in KS4 enrolment after the line opened. The effect is driven mainly by non-disadvantaged pupils. I find no evidence of improved attainment, although GCSE entry intensity increased modestly. Mechanism evidence points to the effect is changed through the variation in housing price and the accessibility gains were capitalised into local housing markets. Enrolment gains were also larger among schools with higher spare capacity and were not concentrated in highly rated schools. The findings show that transport infrastructure can increase school enrolment near newly connected areas, but that the benefits of improved accessibility are filtered through residential sorting and school capacity constraints.
